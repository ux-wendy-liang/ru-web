Role User Researcher, UX & UI Designer
Duration 8 Weeks
Team 1 PM, 1 Designer, 7 Engineers
The Context

A Dashboard Everyone Visits, But Nobody Can Use Deeply

LinkedIn's internal data visualization tool (Retina) helps product managers, engineers, and leadership track product metrics. The dashboard was one of the most visited pages — hundreds of internal users relied on it daily to make product decisions.

But there was a problem hiding in plain sight: users couldn't filter or explore data directly on the dashboard. Every time a PM wanted to drill into the data, they had to click into individual charts, apply filters there, then mentally piece together the story across multiple views.

The result? PMs couldn't self-serve. They constantly asked data scientists to pull numbers for them — turning data creators into "human filters."

The Turning Point

Engineering Had a Solution. I Thought We Were Solving the Wrong Problem.

Some users had requested tab-level filters, and the engineering manager proposed building exactly that — a quick win to address the specific request.

The proposed fix: Add tab-level filters to let users refine data within each tab.

On the surface, it made sense. But something didn't sit right with me.

I had been observing how people actually used the dashboard — not just what they requested. Tab-level filters would solve one symptom, but I suspected deeper usability issues were driving the frustration. If we built the wrong solution, we'd waste 7 engineers' time and the problems would persist.

My design judgment: Before building anything, we needed to understand why users were struggling — not just what they asked for. User requests are symptoms; I wanted to find the root cause.

Convincing Stakeholders to Invest in Research

This wasn't an easy sell. Engineering wanted to ship fast. I made the case to both the PM and engineering manager: "We have 8 engineers ready to build. Let me take 2 weeks to make sure we're building the right thing."

I framed it as risk reduction — the cost of 2 weeks of research was far lower than the cost of shipping a solution that didn't solve the real problem. They agreed.

Research

Uncovering What Users Actually Needed

I designed and led a research study with two distinct user groups — because I hypothesized that dashboard creators (data scientists) and dashboard consumers (PMs, leadership) had fundamentally different pain points.

What I Found: Two Sides of the Same Problem

Data Consumer (PM, Leadership)

Filters are too technical

Users had to select a "dataset" before applying filters — a concept they didn't understand. They just wanted to filter by "country" or "date."

Data Consumer (PM, Leadership)

Filters behave unpredictably

A filter might affect one chart but not others. Users had no way to know the scope of their filters, leading to confusion and mistrust in the data.

Data Creator (Data Scientist)

Can't unify similar filters

Two charts might both have a "country" filter, but named differently in their datasets. No way to link them — creating inconsistent behavior.

Data Creator (Data Scientist)

Became "human filters"

Because consumers couldn't self-serve, data scientists spent hours answering ad-hoc questions instead of doing analysis work.

The Golden Insight

"

Retina is too technical for consumer self-discovery, which often leads to the producer being a human filter.

— From user research interviews

This single quote reframed the entire project. The problem wasn't missing tab filters — the problem was that the tool was designed for data scientists, not for the PMs and leaders who needed it most. We needed to create two distinct experiences: one for creators to build intuitive dashboards, and one for consumers to explore data without technical knowledge.

The Solution

Two Experiences, One Dashboard

Based on my research, I proposed separating the dashboard into two distinct modes — a fundamental shift from the original "one-size-fits-all" approach. I presented this direction to the PM and engineering team with my research findings as evidence, and gained alignment.

Reflection

What I Learned

Challenge the brief, not just execute it. The original ask was "add tab filters." By questioning whether that was the right solution, I uncovered a deeper structural problem that changed the project's scope and impact.

Research is how you earn trust. I couldn't just say "I think the engineering solution is wrong." I needed evidence. The user interviews gave me concrete quotes and patterns that convinced stakeholders to change direction.

Separate the users to serve them better. The key insight — that creators and consumers have fundamentally different needs — seems obvious in hindsight. But it took deliberate research to see it, because the existing tool treated everyone the same.
